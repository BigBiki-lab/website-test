A lens is a piece of transparent material shaped to refract light in particular ways.

Lenses form images by refracting light and changing its direction.

There are two main types of lens - converging and diverging.

Converging lens

A converging lens bulges outwards in the middle.

It causes parallel rays of light to be brought together (converge) at the focal point.

They are sometimes called convex lenses.

Diverging lens

A diverging lens caves inwards.

It causes parallel rays of light to spread out (diverge).

They are sometimes called concave lenses.

Power of lens

The power of lens describes how much it bends light that passes through it.

A more powerful lens is more curved and bends the light more.

The power of a lens describes how much it bends light passing through it.

Real image

The kind of image formed by a converging lens depends on where the object is.

A converging lens can be used to focus rays of light onto a screen. An image that can be projected onto a screen in this way is called a real image.

Real images can only be formed by light rays that come together.

This image is also inverted (upside down) and smaller than the object.

Virtual image

An object close to a converging lens will form a virtual image.

It is called virtual because it cannot be projected onto a screen. The image appears to be on the same side of the lens as the object, and its upright and magnified.

A magnifying glass is a converging lens.

Diverging lenses always produce virtual images that are the same way up, much smaller and closer to the lens than the object.
